TOKYO, 2026

A city held by bears. Three must decide: who becomes Doraemon and dies to save Japan.
THE SETUP
Tokyo, 2026. Bears control the city. Three people—Momoko, Yuki, and Kanji—must decide who will become Doraemon and die. They have two hours.
PRIME MINISTER
Momoko Aoki. Yuki Tanaka. Kanji Hayashi. All born at 1:11 a.m., January 1st, 2001. The Bear King demands one of you become Doraemon.
KANJI
Why? What does our birth time have to do with bears?
PRIME MINISTER
The bears believe in a prophecy. They will follow Doraemon back to the forests. But the one who goes... will not return.
THE ULTIMATUM
YUKI
And if we refuse?
PRIME MINISTER
Then Tokyo falls tonight. Japan ceases to exist.
He places an alarm clock on the table. The loud TICKING begins.
PRIME MINISTER (CONT'D)
You have two hours.
He exits. The door seals.
KANJI'S OFFER - RAMADAN
KANJI
I… I should go.
MOMOKO
Why, Kanji?
KANJI
Because it is Ramadan. Well, it was. It ended last week. But… the spirit remains. It is about self-discipline, sacrifice, and empathy for those who suffer. For thirty days, we abstain to draw closer to God, to purify ourselves, to remember the needy. Now… look outside. All of Japan is needy. All are suffering. If the ultimate act of Ramadan is to give of oneself completely to alleviate suffering, in obedience to God's will… then perhaps this is my test. A final, terrible sadaqah.
He speaks with passion, but his hands tremble.
YUKI'S RESPONSE - CHENGFU
YUKI
That is a noble impulse, Kanji. But is it wisdom? Or is it fear speaking, dressed as piety? Let us think, not just feel. In my tradition—the Daoist part of me—we speak of Chengfu.
MOMOKO
"Returning to simplicity"?
YUKI
Yes. But more. It is the burden of one's innate nature, the karma of your very being before you even act. The weight you are born to carry. My Buddhist side would call it vāsanā, the karmic imprints. We three were all born at the same, absurdly specific moment. 1:11 am, January 1st, 2001. Why? What is the Chengfu of that moment? What is the collective burden we share, simply by being here, born then?
MOMOKO'S DILEMMA - PROBLEM OF EVIL
MOMOKO
You speak of understanding the current. But my faith speaks of a God who is the source of the river itself. And this… this horror of the bears, the slaughter, the collapse… it forces the oldest question to the surface. The one we call the problem of evil.
She stands, walking to the window.
MOMOKO (CONT'D)
If God is all-good, He would want to stop this. If He is all-powerful, He could stop this. If He is all-knowing, He would have foreseen and prevented it. Yet the bears rule Japan. So, where is God? Is He not good? Not powerful? Or… is this part of some plan we cannot fathom?
KANJI'S VIEW - TEST FROM GOD
KANJI
It is a test! Like the story of Ibrahim and Ismail. God tested their obedience. This is a test for Japan, for us!
MOMOKO
Is it? To allow children to be torn apart by bears as a test? That does not sound like love. It sounds like cruelty. And yet… my faith insists: God is love. Not just loving, but Love itself. The very essence.
EXPLANATION OF EVIL - GOD IS LOVE
MOMOKO (CONT'D)
Love needs freedom to be real. God gave us—and maybe even the bears—true freedom of choice. They chose to leave the forest. They chose violence. If God stepped in to force a different choice, we'd just be puppets. You can't force love on a puppet. That freedom is worth the terrible price of their choice.
YUKI
So God values the principle of freedom more than the immediate prevention of suffering?
MOMOKO
You're right, it's not indifference. Love doesn't watch from a distance—it enters the suffering, like Christ on the cross. So if I go, it won't be to fulfill a myth or please a god. It will be an act of love. A choice to enter a broken world and share its pain, to save others from it. Not because I have to, but because true love, when faced with evil, chooses sacrifice.
YUKI ON KARMA
YUKI
Your explanation about evil makes sense from a distance. It's about the stage, but not the actors. My view focuses on Karma—the action itself. It's about cause and effect. Every action plant a seed. The bears' violence is the fruit of many causes: our invasion of their home, the changing world. Us being here is also a result of past causes. The choice we make now will plant a new seed with huge consequences for the future.
She pours water from a pitcher into three cups.
INTENTION AND KARMA
YUKI (CONT'D)
The reason why we go matters as much as going itself. If Kanji goes out of fear, that action is rooted in fear. If Momoko goes in loving sacrifice, it may carry a hidden pride. If I go, I must ask: am I trying to truly end the cycle of suffering, or just running from my own? Karma is precise. The intention plants the seed.
KANJI
So we must not only decide who, but with what mind? That's impossible to purify!
BREAKING THE CYCLE
YUKI
It is the work of a lifetime. We have two hours. The point is, we must be ruthlessly honest. This is not about earning a reward. In Buddhism, sacrifice for reward is still a transaction, binding you to the cycle. The ultimate goal is to act with such selfless compassion that you break the cycle. For the sake of all beings. Including the bears.
MOMOKO
Compassion… for the bears?
YUKI
They are suffering too. They are driven by a hunger they do not understand, a search for a mythical ruler. They are trapped in aggression and fear. They are part of the sentient world suffering from ignorance. A Bodhisattva would have compassion for them as well. So the act of going… if done with that ultimate, impartial compassion… could generate immense, liberating merit. Not for me, but for the situation itself.
DOUBTS AND INTROSPECTION
KANJI
I offered to go. But now… your words confuse me. Is my offer true submission, or just a desperate attempt to make sense of the chaos? To force God's hand?
YUKI
It is. And it touches on Chengfu again. Your innate nature, Kanji, is passionate and devout. That is your burden and your gift. To act against it would be a violence to yourself. To act solely from its fever pitch might be unwise. You must find its still center.
An alarm clock ticks loudly. An hour has passed.
WHICH STORY WILL DIE?
MOMOKO
Perhaps we are asking the wrong question. We're asking "Who should die?" as if it's a philosophical contest. Maybe the question is "Who can do this thing, and what will it mean?"
MOMOKO (CONT'D)
Exactly. Kanji, if you go, your act becomes one of Islamic submission to the Divine Decree, a final, profound Ramadan sacrifice. It will be remembered as a Muslim act.
MOMOKO (CONT'D)
Yuki, if you go, your act becomes one of Buddhist-Daoist compassion and karmic resolution. A release from cycles.
MOMOKO (CONT'D)
And if I go… it becomes a Christian act of sacrificial love. A participation in the divine response to evil.
THE DECISION
KANJI
I have been thinking. About your God is love, Momoko. And about submission. In Islam, God is Al-Wadud, the Loving. His will is an expression of that love. My offer… it was not trust. It was a frantic bargain. "Take me so this ends." That is not loving submission. That is terror. I must… I must withdraw my offer.
YUKI
And I have been thinking of Karma. If I go seeking to be a Bodhisattva, the very seeking plants the seed of ego. The 'I' who will be a hero. The 'I' who will generate merit. It is a trap. True compassion is without a self who is compassionate.
YUKI (CONT'D)
You, Momoko. You spoke of love not as a feeling, but as the nature of God. As a person. An act. That… feels less karmically sticky. It is about the nature of the act itself, not its future fruit for the actor.
MOMOKO'S CHOICE
MOMOKO
(Tears welling) I am afraid. I do not want to die. The thought of those bears… it is a primal horror.
YUKI
Of course. But your fear does not negate your nature. It contextualizes it. A sacrifice without fear is no sacrifice. It is just a transaction.
Momoko looks out the window. The sun is lower.
MOMOKO
If I do this… it is not because I believe the Shinto master's myth. It is because I believe in the God who is love. And if Love has a place in this bear-ridden hell, it is beside the victim, becoming the victim, to stop the victimizing. Not by power, but by solidarity. Even if it looks like a fool in a Doraemon costume.
MOMOKO (CONT'D)
I will go.
THE SACRIFICE
A buzzer sounds. The two hours are up. Momoko stands before the Bear King in the Doraemon costume. She removes the head, revealing her human face.
MOMOKO
The search is over. I am here. Now… let your people go. Go back to the forests. Leave the cities. The killing ends now.
The Bear King roars a command. The bears turn and begin their exodus north.
YUKI (V.O., whispering)
Chengfu fulfilled.
KANJI (V.O., through tears)
God is love.
MOMOKO (V.O., a gentle whisper)
And love has driven out the fear.
[SCENE END]
